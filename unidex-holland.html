Category Opportunity Brief — South Indian & Kerala Ambient Food
Your Current Range

Unidex Holland carries one of the most comprehensive exotic food portfolios in the Benelux, with a well-established South Asian shelf that includes TRS across herbs, spices, and cooking essentials; Bikaji across 37 SKUs of North Indian namkeens and sweet snacks; Gits with 30 ready-meal and mix SKUs; National pickles prominently featured; Dabur, Mother's Recipe, Humza, and Horlicks rounding out the broader Indian category. It is a genuinely strong foundation across North Indian and pan-Indian ambient food.

The Gap We Noticed

Your Indian range does not include a single Kerala or South Indian ambient snack brand. Banana chips, tapioca chips, jackfruit chips, Kerala mixture, murukku — this entire sub-category is absent, despite being the fastest-growing segment within the South Indian diaspora ambient food market in the Netherlands and Benelux.

This is not an oversight on your part. TRS, Bikaji, Gits, and National each have a defined focus that does not extend into Kerala ambient snacks. It is a structural white space that your current partner brands were never designed to fill.

The same gap applies to South Indian pickles (mango varieties, lime pickle, ginger pickle from the Kerala tradition) and South Indian pantry staples like coconut oil, chutney powders, and payasam mixes — none of which appear in the range of the Indian brands you carry today.

Why This Category Moves

The Netherlands has a well-established South Asian diaspora community, including a significant Keralite and broader South Indian population concentrated in cities such as Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, and Eindhoven. This community purchases ambient food with high frequency and strong brand loyalty — but only when the products taste authentic. They will not substitute a North Indian snack for a Kerala one. The purchase intent is category-specific.

Gramarhein's own customer feedback confirms this repeatedly: buyers specifically say the products taste exactly as they do back home. This is what drives repeat purchase and builds the shelf velocity that makes the category worth holding. It is not a novelty buy.

Bikaji covers the Rajasthani and North Indian snack consumer well. Gits covers the convenience meal buyer. Neither brand speaks to the customer looking for banana chips from Calicut or a Kerala mixture that tastes like it came from a Thiruvananthapuram supermarket. That customer is currently going without — or travelling to specialist stores that charge a premium for the same products. Unidex has the distribution infrastructure to bring this to mainstream retail, and Minzelis has the range to fill it.
